History

Author: John Reniel Cruz

Centuries ago, the land that would become Apayao witnessed the migration of indigenous groups, setting the stage for its cultural diversity. Kankanaey tribes from Benguet and Mountain Province, particularly from Loo, Kapangan, and Tadian, joined by Ilocano groups from Pao Sur and Pao Norte of San Fernando, La Union, were among the earliest settlers in this region.

The influx of migrants from Benguet and Mountain Province was driven by the "Buso-buso" tribal conflict, compelling them to seek new lands. Meanwhile, the Ilocano groups ventured into the area in pursuit of better livelihoods.

The shaping of the region continued with the arrival of American colonizers, who established the Mountain Province on August 13, 1908. Apayao, previously part of Cagayan province since the Spanish Period, was incorporated along with Amburayan, Benguet, Bontoc, Ifugao, Kalinga, and Lepanto as sub-provinces of this newly formed province.

The year 1966 marked a significant division, as the expansive Mountain Province was split into four provinces, namely Benguet, Bontoc (renamed "Mountain Province"), Kalinga-Apayao, and Ifugao. Kalinga-Apayao, along with Ifugao, was included among the provinces constituting the Cagayan Valley Region in 1972.

A defining moment occurred on July 15, 1987, with the establishment of the Cordillera Administrative Region, elevating Kalinga-Apayao as one of its provinces. Finally, on February 14, 1995, Kalinga-Apayao was bifurcated into two independent provinces, a decision aimed at better catering to the distinct needs of individual ethnic groups within the region.

The early settlers in Apayao subsisted by cultivating root crops like camote, gabi, and corn. Over time, they constructed terraces, referred to as "Payew" by the Kankanaey and "Payao" by the Ilocanos, for agricultural purposes. The name "Payew" was initially used due to the larger Kankanaey population, although it was officially recorded as Apayao when the area gained recognition as a Barangay.

By the 20th century, the Isneg people became predominant in Apayao. Concentrated primarily in the highland municipalities of Kabugao and Calanasan, most Isneg settlements were found along the upper stretches of the Apayao-Abulug River, while others resided along a significant tributary of the Matalag River in Conner.

Demography

Authors: John Reniel Cruz & Rayne Cristel Axibal

The Apayao people, encompassing diverse subgroups like Isneg, Isnag, Mandaya, Ibulus, Imandaya, Imallod, Itne’g, Kalina’, Apayaw, Iapayaw, Idamma’n, and Abulog, predominantly inhabit the northern Cordillera mountain ranges within Kalinga and Apayao provinces.

This population coexists with various ethnic groups like Kalinga, Itawit, Negrito, and Ilocano farmers. The Apayao populace exhibits at least three distinct types, concentrated notably in municipalities like Pudtol, Kabugao, Kalanasan, and Conner. In lower areas adjoining rivers and floodplains, their agricultural practices involve riverine techniques, including slash-and-burn cropping and wet rice farming. Their diet consists of yams, taro, corn, sweet potatoes, and rice, which holds significance as a prestige crop. Residential setups typically comprise small familial hamlets with sturdy multi-family dwellings.

The political organization among the Apayao people revolves around "mengal," individuals distinguished in economics and leadership. Leadership isn't inherited but earned. Their cultural calendar includes ceremonies like "sayam," often attended by affluent individuals, guided by female ritual specialists who also serve as mediums. A customary feature during ceremonial events involves mengals exhibiting pride near a ritual stone, a defining aspect of these feasts.

Apayao's demographic landscape extends beyond its ethnic diversity to encompass a unique blend of cultural practices and social structures. These communities share territories with other ethnic groups like the Kalinga, Itawit, Negrito, and Ilocano farmers, fostering a mosaic of traditions within the region's northern Cordillera mountain ranges.

Distinct Apayao populations are concentrated across strategic municipalities such as Pudtol, Kabugao, Kalanasan, and Conner, delineating varied cultural practices and livelihood patterns across these areas. Their agricultural techniques, adapted to different terrains, range from riverine agriculture in low-lying regions adjoining rivers and floodplains to slash-and-burn cropping methods. Rice, considered a prestige crop, accompanies staples like yams, taro, corn, and sweet potatoes in their diet, signifying the region's agricultural diversity.

The socio-political structure among the Apayao people revolves around the "mengal," individuals recognized for their economic prowess and leadership qualities. Leadership positions are achieved based on merit rather than inheritance, fostering a dynamic leadership landscape within the community. Cultural festivities, notably the "sayam" ceremonies, serve as cultural touchpoints, attracting participation, particularly from affluent community members. These rituals are led by female ritual specialists who double as mediums, maintaining cultural continuity and spiritual guidance within the community.

Apayao's cultural ethos is punctuated by ceremonial practices intertwined with spiritual significance. At these events, mengals boast near ritual stones, emblematic of their cultural identity and the ceremonial essence of these gatherings. These events serve not just as social gatherings but also as preservers of cultural heritage, reflecting the depth and vibrancy of Apayao's cultural tapestry.

The Apayao demographic landscape is characterized by a rich interplay of customs and traditions, fostering a unique socio-cultural environment. This cultural diversity is evident in the region's cohabitation of various ethnic groups, including the Apayao people alongside the Kalinga, Itawit, Negrito, and Ilocano farmers, contributing to the multifaceted tapestry of cultural practices and beliefs in the northern Cordillera mountain ranges.

Distinct Apayao populations concentrated in municipalities like Pudtol, Kabugao, Kalanasan, and Conner exhibit diverse agricultural techniques shaped by geographical factors. Riverine agriculture and slash-and-burn cropping techniques prevail in different terrains, reflecting a deep-rooted connection between the community and its environment. The significance of rice as a prestige crop, alongside staples like yams, taro, corn, and sweet potatoes, underscores the agricultural diversity and resilience of these communities.

The socio-political framework within Apayao centers around the "mengal," individuals distinguished for their economic prowess and leadership qualities. This non-hereditary leadership structure fosters a dynamic leadership landscape, promoting merit-based ascendancy and adaptability within the community. Cultural celebrations, especially the "sayam" ceremonies, serve as cultural anchors, attracting active participation from influential members and guided by female ritual specialists who play vital roles in preserving traditions and spiritual guidance.

Apayao's cultural identity thrives through ceremonial practices intertwined with cultural significance. The display of pride by mengals near ritual stones during these gatherings symbolizes the community's deep-rooted cultural heritage and the symbolic essence of these occasions. These ceremonial events serve as both social gatherings and preservers of cultural legacy, embodying the depth and richness of Apayao's cultural identity.

Geography

Authors: John Reniel Cruz & Mark Kevin Cawilan

Apayao, situated in the Cordillera Administrative Region (CAR) of the Philippines, lies in the northern expanse of Luzon Island. Kabugao serves as its capital. The province is entirely landlocked, bordered by Cabugao to the north and east, Cagayan to the west, and Abra and Ilocos Norte to the east. To the south, it shares its boundaries with Kalinga. Until 1995, Kalinga and Apayao comprised a unified province, known as Kalinga-Apayao. However, recognizing distinct regional needs and diverse cultures, they were divided into separate provinces. Apayao spans 4,413.35 square kilometers (1,704.00 square miles), boasting an extensive landscape.

The province's topography features rugged terrains and mountainous landscapes, characteristic of the Cordillera mountain range. It's traversed by rivers and streams, notably the Apayao River, serving as a prominent geographical feature and a vital resource for the province. This river system sustains agriculture and various economic activities within the region. Apayao's terrain comprises valleys, plateaus, and forests, contributing to its diverse ecosystem.

Within its boundaries lie natural reserves and protected areas, preserving biodiversity and indigenous flora and fauna. The protected areas include the Calanasan Forest Reserve, a habitat for various wildlife species. Apayao's climate is generally characterized by distinct wet and dry seasons, typical of tropical regions. The dry season typically spans from November to April, while the wet season prevails from May to October.

Apayao's economy primarily revolves around agriculture, with rice, corn, and root crops among its key agricultural products. Livestock farming also plays a significant role in the province's economy. Small-scale industries, such as weaving, handicrafts, and woodcarving, contribute to the local economy. The province also taps into mining activities, primarily for gold and limestone, contributing to its economic landscape.

The province's demographic composition encompasses various indigenous groups, each with unique cultural traditions and practices. Indigenous communities, including the Isnag, Isneg, and Kalinga, form an integral part of Apayao's cultural tapestry. These communities preserve their traditions, dialects, and customary practices, contributing to the province's cultural diversity.

Languages

Authors: John Reniel Cruz, Jerrick Zeus Tabilin, & Brendell Nicholas

The Apayao people possess a diverse linguistic landscape, comprising several languages significant to their cultural identity and heritage.

One such language is Isneg, also known as Isnag or Maragat, predominantly spoken in the Cordillera Administrative Region within Northern Luzon. Its roots trace back to the Austronesian language family, with influences across the Pacific region. Isneg is prevalent in parts of Apayao, Cagayan, Abra, and Ilocos Norte Provinces. It incorporates unique linguistic features, including a 22-letter alphabet, characterized by a glottal stop marker denoted by the symbol "ʔ." This marker, used after consonants, distinguishes between words with similar spellings but varying meanings, enriching communication nuances.

Ibanag, an Austronesian language native to the Ibanag people, thrives primarily in the Cagayan Valley region and northern Luzon, including Apayao. Despite its fewer speakers compared to some Philippine languages, it holds cultural and linguistic importance within its native territory. Its relation to other Philippine languages accentuates the country's linguistic diversity.

Itawis, closely connected to Ibanag and Ilocano, is a Northern Philippine dialect spoken by the Itawis people inhabiting the Cagayan Valley province. Itawis introduces distinct consonants like z, f, j (pronounced like dy but sounding like j), and v, distinguishing it from other Philippine dialects. Its linguistic and cultural affinity with Ibanag fosters mutual understanding, allowing speakers of both dialects to comprehend each other easily.

Kankanaey, an Austronesian language, flourishes among the Kankanaey people in the Benguet province of Luzon. Predominantly spoken in the Cordillera region, it is an integral part of the indigenous languages in the area. Traditionally oral, Kankanaey possesses a rich heritage of oral literature, storytelling, and song. Efforts have been underway to develop a writing system, marking a transition from its predominantly oral tradition.

These languages embody the cultural heritage and diversity of the Apayao people, reflecting historical and linguistic intricacies that enrich the region's cultural tapestry. Their significance lies not only in linguistic communication but also in preserving the indigenous identity and heritage of the communities they represent.

Culture

Authors: John Reniel Cruz, Jeffrey Takinan, & Azailu Jendrick Bautista

Among the Isnags of Apayao, spirituality remains a prominent aspect of their cultural fabric. Much akin to other Igorot communities, the Isnags exhibit a deep-rooted belief in spiritual forces. Central to their spirituality is the reverence for God, referred to as Alawagan, and various spirits inhabiting their environment. Isnags attribute their abundant environmental resources to these spiritual entities. Rituals form a crucial part of Isnag spirituality, performed at specific times using symbolic materials to impart effectiveness and binding power. These rituals serve diverse purposes, including healing—addressing physical, spiritual, or psychological ailments.

The Isnags' rituals revolve significantly around their belief in environmental spirits, dictating their way of life. A comprehensive interview conducted with a tribal leader, Sabina Abgao from Kumao, Kabugao, Apayao, in April 2008 shed light on the features and significance of these rituals. Subsequently, the article underwent review and validation by Director Henry Aliten, a native of Calanasan, Apayao. Additional validation occurred through a focus group discussion and the assessment of an article titled "Apayao: Home of the Isnag Tribe" by Miguel Vasquez, published in the Baguio Midland Courier on April 27, 2008. The discussed rituals, particularly Say-am and Pildap, are essential facets of Isnag cultural practices.

Say-am, a crucial religious ritual among the Isnags, serves as a community affair attended by villagers from neighboring areas. It operates as a cleansing ritual, especially if hosted by those undergoing a period of mourning. This ceremony marks the conclusion of mourning, allowing the harvest and consumption of previously taboo plants. Say-am, also observed as a thanksgiving ritual, often hosted by affluent Isnags, emphasizes gratitude and sharing of blessings. The celebration involves prayer, dance, feasting, and the slaughter of large animals like cattle and pigs.

Pildap, another communal affair akin to Say-am but less elaborate, involves the sacrifice of smaller animals like dogs or chickens. Its purpose extends to signifying events, seeking healing, requesting prayers for health, prosperity, or the inauguration of a new household. Moreover, Pildap marks significant life events like a man's first adult haircut or serves as a healing ritual for an ailing family member. Additionally, it involves community-based assistance during agricultural activities like house construction, rice planting, or harvesting, following the principle of Bayanihan, wherein volunteers help without compensation.

Both Say-am and Pildap hold paramount significance among the Isnags, functioning as platforms to express gratitude to the divine for health, resources, and life. These rituals also serve as a communal bond, uniting individuals through shared food, resources, prayers, and the reinforcement of friendships, cooperation, and unity within the community.
